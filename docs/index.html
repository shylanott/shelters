By Amber Thomas

September 2019

If you’re looking to add a new furry friend to your family, you may be encouraged to “adopt not shop”.

But where do adoptable dogs in come from?

This question can be complicated, but let’s start with a dog named Bleu.

Bleu was one of 1,265 dogs available for adoption in Washington on PetFinder, June 28, 2019. But Bleu didn’t originate in Washington. According to his description, he was brought into the state from a shelter in California.

On the day that Bleu was available, 27% of the adoptable dogs in Washington came into the local rescues and shelters from out of state.

Bleu was one of 1,265 dogs available for adoption in Washington on PetFinder, June 28, 2019. And, like all the other adoptable dogs in the state, Bleu originated in Washington.

But Washington is one of only 13 states that didn’t import adoptable dogs from other states or countries. In fact, Washington exported 55 adoptable pups to other states.

So, if these dogs weren’t strays or surrendered by their owners locally, where did they come from?

Note: Each dog above is depicted by an icon that is most visually similar to the presumed primary breed of the dog. Each import into the state was determined by parsing the PetFinder description for each animal. More details in the Methods section.

Shelters and rescue organizations have been moving animals around the country, reportedly, since the mid 2000’s, with some pointing to Hurricane Katrina as the primary catalyst. Evacuation plans during the detrimental 2005 storm didn’t account for moving or accommodating pets or stray animals so an estimated 250,000 animals were left behind.

Louisiana’s SPCA and other rescue organizations from across the country worked to save an estimated 15,000 animals during the aftermath of the storm. Then these animals were transported all across the country to wait to be reunited with their families or to eventually find a new one.

Since then, new legislation has been enacted to ensure that pets are a planned part of evacuations and rescues still work during emergencies to provide safety for animals. But these days, the transport of animals goes far behind emergency response.

In 2018, the ASPCA reports that they relocated over 40,000 animals from areas of “high homeless pet overpopulation” to “areas with a higher demand for adoptable dogs”. Although our data is a snapshot of animal movement, representing all the adoptable animals available across the country on a single day, we can see geographically where animals are exported from and imported to.

In New England and Pacific Northwest states, rescue dogs are in demand, but sometimes in short supply. I’ve seen several reports of families wanting to adopt a dog, but the ones that were local and met their needs in both size or temperament were gone too quickly.

In southern states, it’s often the opposite. Due in part to more lax spay and neuter programs, poverty, a warmer climate expanding dogs’ breeding window, and in some cases, a different perspective on the role pets play in their families, stray dogs are very common in some areas of the south. And the shelters and rescues only have so much space to put them.

As the “no-kill” movement has gained momentum, euthanizing animals in shelters as a way of freeing up space has declined dramatically. A 2017 study estimates that out of the 5.5 million dogs brought into shelters in the US annually, 780,000 (1.5%) of them are euthanized. (Due to legitimate health and behavior concerns, this number is unlikely to ever reach zero.) So animals are moved around the country to fill a need.

While there are still plenty of great dogs that need homes, as pet owners look to rescues and shelters for their next companion and state legislations require that pet stores only sell animals from rescues, some are starting to ask if everyone who wants a rescue dog can even find one.

Cue bringing in dogs from outside the US. Out of all of the dogs we identified as imports into a new state, 12% of them came from outside the US. According to our snapshot data review, South Korea exported the 8th most dogs to states across the US, exporting 20 dogs more than Kentucky and nearly 30 more than West Virginia.

The movement of animals both domestically and internationally has been met with a mixed bag of responses. For some, a dog in need anywhere deserves a good life and home and they’ll do anything in their power to see that happen. They volunteer countless hours to driving dogs across the country in search of a “forever home”. Pilots volunteer their time to fly animals, or they help to clean up and train newly imported dogs for their new families. They’re doing hard work with the dogs in mind.

For others, there are some serious risks to this practice to consider, such as the transfer of diseases and parasites to new areas. Earlier this year, Rhode Island’s state veterinarian told Pew that “From a pure health perspective, we shouldn’t be doing this...But it’s political now. People think they’re doing a good thing. They want to do this.” After Rhode Island’s incidence rate for the deadly (and contagious) canine parvovirus went from two incidents per year to two per week in 2012, they have a right to be worried.

Heartworm rates in Seattle-dwelling dogs has skyrocketed over 300% between 2012 and 2017. And while climate change and a lack of prevention may have played some role in that jump, Dr. Brian Joseph, the State Veterinarian at the Washington State Department of Agriculture, told SeattleDogSpot that rescues (either accidentally or willingly) bringing heartwork-positive dogs into the state could also share some of the blame.

Within the past 6 months alone, the Center for Disease Control has put a temporary ban on importing dogs from Egypt due to concerns of canine rabies (a strand eliminated from the US in 2007) and a new strain of the highly contagious canine distemper virus was found on a dog imported to Canada from South Korea.

Transporting unhealthy dogs is, of course, not the intent of reputable rescue organizations. There are plenty that prioritize the health of the animals and require validated health records before moving any animals. But since there is no regulation (or even central data collection) on animals moved within the country, less-than-reputable “rescue organizations” have emerged. Often known as “retail rescues” these organizations buy dogs from “puppy mills”, dog auctions, or other unsafe conditions, move them across the country, and sell them to unsuspecting families as “rescues”.

And so, we find ourselves in the murky gray area of moving adoptable dogs. The data we’ve presented here is, again, only a snapshot. It represents one day’s worth of adoptable dogs in the US that were listed on PetFinder. Without a central database, it’s impossible for us to know how many animals are moved across state lines or imported into the country, but this is one attempt to take a peek at a complex situation.

Methods

Using the PetFinder API, details about all 57,190 dogs available for adoption on June 28, 2019 were collected. Since PetFinder does not provide an entry field for an animal’s location before arriving at its current organization, I parsed the text of each pet’s “description”. I started by limiting text to anything that came after the word “from” but before the word “to”, or after “located in”. I then analyzed the remaining text using entity recognition from the spacyR package. I manually checked the data for anything mislabeled. In all, 3,361 dogs were described as having originated in places different from where they were listed for adoption. We consider this an underestimate since any listings for animals from a vague region (e.g., “the south”) without listing a specific state were not included in our analysis.
